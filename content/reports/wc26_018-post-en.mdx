## Match Narrative

Norway delivered a commanding 2-0 victory over Iraq at Gillette Stadium in their Group I opener, with Erling Haaland the dominant figure in a display of physical and technical authority. Martin Ødegaard orchestrated Norway's patient build-up from the first whistle, his fluid movement between the lines denying Iraq's midfield any chance to establish rhythm. Iraq, organized in a compact 4-3-3, defended resolutely for the opening twenty minutes but were overrun by Norway's relentless pressing and precision passing. The breakthrough arrived in the 28th minute when Haaland rose magnificently above Saman Goran and Rebin Sulaka to power a header from Omar Elabdellaoui's right-flank delivery into the top corner — a finish that gave Jalal Hassan no chance. Iraq's structure crumbled visibly in the aftermath, their midfielders drawn too high in attempts to respond, leaving dangerous channels behind for the Scandinavians to exploit.

## Tactical Breakdown

Norway's 4-3-3 under Ståle Solbakken functioned as a high-pressing machine, with Alexander Sørloth and Haaland pressing Iraq's central defenders aggressively from the front. Sander Berge's control of the defensive midfield zone was authoritative — he intercepted three Iraqi attacks in the first half alone and consistently recycled possession to Ødegaard, who picked passes through the lines with devastating effect. Iraq's plan had been to absorb pressure and use Hammam Ibrahim's pace on the counter, but Norway's defensive line sat so high and so compactly that the Iraqi wingers were starved of space. Ødegaard's second-half goal in the 71st minute typified the evening: a quick exchange of passes with Mohamed Elyounoussi on the left opened a corridor, Ødegaard delayed the finish beautifully before stroking the ball low past Hassan into the far corner, killing the contest entirely and underlining Norway's technical superiority.

## Key Moments

Haaland's 28th-minute header defined the match. The Manchester City striker's timing and elevation were extraordinary — both Iraqi center-backs were beaten in the air despite reasonable positioning — and the power of the finish spoke to Haaland's unique physical gifts. Iraq's attempts to respond were disjointed; Bashar Resan struggled to impose himself in midfield against the physicality of Berge, and Aymen Hussein's forays forward too often resulted in possession turning over in dangerous areas. By the 60th minute, with Norway controlling possession at 60% and Iraq showing signs of physical fatigue in the humid Massachusetts evening, the game was over as a contest. Ødegaard's 71st-minute finish was aesthetically the better of the two goals — a delayed, low side-foot execution that showcased his intelligence and composure under pressure.

## Aftermath

Norway's comprehensive victory positions them as genuine contenders in Group I, alongside France. Haaland's opener was his twelfth international goal in major tournament football and demonstrated that, even at the World Cup's highest level, the Norwegian target man remains uniquely difficult to defend aerially. Ødegaard's creative influence — five key passes, 89% passing accuracy — was the engine that drove Norway's control, and his goal typified the fluid, interconnected attacking style Solbakken has cultivated. Iraq will be deeply disappointed: their defensive organization was solid early but their lack of quality in possession transitions eventually told. The three yellow cards accumulated through frustration reflect the mounting pressure they faced. They must regroup significantly if they are to accumulate points in subsequent Group I fixtures against tougher-looking competition.
