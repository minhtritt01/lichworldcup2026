## Tactical Analysis

Spain enters as overwhelming favorites and will likely deploy their familiar 4-3-3 formation, with Rodri anchoring midfield and Pedri operating in a secondary playmaking role. The focal point of Spanish control will be possession-based buildup through the centerbacks (Laporte, Le Normand) and full-back circulation. Carvajal and Balde will push high, creating width while seeking overloads in wide channels. Cape Verde will adopt a compact 4-4-2 or 5-4-1 defensive shape, prioritizing compactness and shape over pressing intensity. The battle zone emerges in Spain's left flank, where Balde's attacking runs meet Cape Verde's defensive organization. Spain's weakness—transitional vulnerability and occasional ponderous buildup—offers Cape Verde counter-attacking opportunities through Jovane Cabral's pace. However, Spain's defensive solidity under Laporte's leadership should limit these chances. Midfield dominance will be Spain's primary advantage; Rodri's work rate and technical security will overwhelm Cape Verde's press.

## Form & Momentum

Spain's draw against Iraq represents a minor setback but masks underlying control—the Spanish side dominated possession and created numerous chances without clinical finishing. This result provides psychological motivation for a statement victory. Cape Verde's 1-0 win over Bermuda represents a significant achievement but came against a weaker opponent; the team will face an exponential jump in quality. The disparity in resources, infrastructure, and player caliber heavily favors Spain, who enter as Copa del Rey and La Liga powerhouses. Cape Verde lacks European club experience among most players, limiting their exposure to elite tactical frameworks. Spain's tournament pedigree and recent confidence in possession-based systems should translate to growing momentum, while Cape Verde faces a reality check. The narrative advantage belongs entirely to Spain, seeking redemption after the Iraq draw.
